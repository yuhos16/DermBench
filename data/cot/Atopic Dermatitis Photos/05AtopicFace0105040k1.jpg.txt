Differential diagnosis:
- **Atopic Dermatitis (AD):** This is the leading contender. Facial involvement (especially cheeks) is extremely common in infantile AD. The combination of acute inflammation (right cheek), chronic dryness/scale (left cheek, perioral), and secondary infection (crusts) is classic. Perioral involvement due to saliva/drooling/irritation is also frequent in AD infants. Sparing the lip border fits.
- **Irritant Contact Dermatitis:** Could explain the perioral findings due to saliva/food, but doesn't typically cause the prominent, bilateral cheek lesions seen here.
- **Seborrheic Dermatitis:** Can affect the face in infants ("cradle cap"), but usually involves the scalp, eyebrows, nasolabial folds more prominently with greasy scales, and less intense inflammation/crusting than seen here.
- **Impetigo:** Explains the crusts on the right cheek, but doesn't account for the bilateral, symmetric, dry/scaly patches elsewhere. Likely secondary.
- **Perioral Dermatitis:** Uncommon in infants; typically shows papules/pustules immediately around the mouth, which aren't described here. The dryness/scaling on cheeks isn't typical.
- **Psoriasis:** Less common in infants on the face; usually has thicker, more silvery scales and well-demarcated plaques.

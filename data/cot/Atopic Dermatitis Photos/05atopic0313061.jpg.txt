3. **Pathological Features**:

   - **Primary Lesions**: The widespread redness and small reddish bumps, along with a dry and scaly texture, align well with the eczematous inflammation typically seen in atopic dermatitis. This crusting may also suggest a secondary bacterial infection (impetiginization). Additionally, lichenification, characterized by thickened skin with prominent lines, points to chronic rubbing or scratching, a hallmark of long-standing atopic dermatitis, especially in flexural areas like the antecubital fossa.
   - **Chronicity**: The presence of lichenification confirms that this is not an acute condition but rather a chronic or recurrent one, which is typical for atopic dermatitis.

4. **Differential Diagnosis**: 
   - **Psoriasis** could be considered, but it typically presents with thicker, silvery scales and well-demarcated plaques, which are not described here.
   - **Contact dermatitis** is usually more localized to areas of exposure and does not typically present with the widespread features noted.
   - **Fungal infections** often show annular lesions, which do not match the described morphology.
   - The combination of the morphology (redness, bumps, scaling), distribution (extensor surfaces), and chronicity (lichenification) strongly favors atopic dermatitis over these alternatives.

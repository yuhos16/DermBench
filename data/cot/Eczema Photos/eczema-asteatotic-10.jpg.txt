Next, I consider common differentials for this presentation on the lower legs: 

1. **Psoriasis:** Silvery scale is classic, but psoriasis plaques are usually thicker, more sharply demarcated, and often symmetrical. The diffuse redness here, combined with deep fissures and a polygonal pattern, is less typical. Psoriasis scale is typically brighter silvery-white, not yellowish. 

2. **Stasis Dermatitis:** Common on lower legs, with redness and scaling. However, it's usually associated with significant edema (swelling), varicose veins, and often more oozing/crusting, which aren't described here. Hyperpigmentation (due to hemosiderin) is common, but the fissuring and polygonal pattern are less characteristic. 

3. **Nummular Eczema:** Presents as coin-shaped plaques with scaling, but the description here is more diffuse and linear (along the leg), not discrete plaques. Fissuring isn't a hallmark. 

4. **Asteatotic Eczema (Eczema Craquelé):** This fits exceptionally well. It's characterized by: 
   * **Location:** Predilection for the shins/lower legs in older adults. 
   * **Appearance:** Diffuse dryness, fine scaling that progresses to large, polygonal plates of scale (the "cracked porcelain" or "dried riverbed" appearance). 
   * **Color:** Scaling often described as fine, bran-like, or having a cracked, polygonal pattern, which can appear whitish or slightly yellowish. 
   * **Fissures:** Deep, painful cracks are a hallmark due to extreme dehydration and loss of skin lipids. 
   * **Erythema:** Background redness from inflammation. 
   * **Lichenification:** Thickening occurs due to chronic rubbing/scratching (itch is common). 
   * **Excoriations & Hyperpigmentation:** Evidence of scratching and chronic inflammation. 

The combination of **diffuse dryness, pronounced polygonal scaling/cracking, fissures, lower leg location, and signs of chronicity (thickening, hyperpigmentation, excoriations)** strongly points away from psoriasis or classic nummular/stasis dermatitis. The "irregular, possibly polygonal pattern" and deep fissures are highly suggestive of the characteristic "crazed" pattern of asteatotic eczema. Environmental factors like low humidity, excessive bathing, or aging skin (reduced sebum production) are common triggers. 

Based on the above analysis, the most likely diagnosis is **asteatotic eczema**.

Next, I consider common palmoplantar dermatoses: 

1. **Chronic hand eczema:** Can cause redness, scaling, cracking, and swelling. However, it often shows vesicles (blisters) in acute phases, is intensely itchy, and scaling is typically less thick and silvery than described. Distribution might be patchier. 

2. **Tinea manuum (fungal infection):** Usually asymmetric, often affects one palm with a "two-feet-one-hand" pattern. Scaling tends to be peripheral/collarette-like, not uniform silvery plaques. 

3. **Psoriasis:** Characterized by well-demarcated erythematous plaques with thick, adherent silvery scales. Palmoplantar psoriasis specifically presents with symmetric involvement, diffuse hyperkeratosis, fissuring, and scaling. Accentuated skin lines, redness, and extension are classic. Koebnerization (seen here on wrist/forearm) is highly supportive. 

4. **Palmoplantar pustulosis:** Features sterile pustules – not described here. 

5. **Keratolysis exfoliativa:** Superficial peeling without significant redness or thickening.

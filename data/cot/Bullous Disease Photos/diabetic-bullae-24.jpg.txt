<think> Differential diagnosis: </think> 
1. **Bullous pemphigoid**: Large tense bullae are classic, but lichenification/scaling are atypical. It usually spares the lower legs initially. 
2. **Allergic contact dermatitis/irritant dermatitis**: Can cause blisters and scaling, but lichenification implies chronicity. The diffuse pattern isn’t classic for a contact allergen. 
3. **Infection (e.g., bullous impetigo)**: Crusting suggests possible infection, but the chronic changes (lichenification) don’t align with acute infections. 
4. **Stasis dermatitis**: Highly likely. Lower leg distribution, edema, erythema, scaling, and lichenification are hallmark. Bullae can occur in severe cases due to chronic venous hypertension ("venous bullae"). 
5. **Diabetic bullae**: Painless bullae on extremities in diabetics, but *no chronic inflammation, scaling, or lichenification* are typical.

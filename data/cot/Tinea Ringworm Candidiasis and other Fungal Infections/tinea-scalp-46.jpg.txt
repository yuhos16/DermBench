1. **Nevus Sebaceus:** This is a strong candidate. It classically presents as a **solitary, hairless, yellow-orange to flesh-colored plaque** with a **cobblestone or pebbled surface**. It's congenital but often becomes more noticeable around puberty. The irregular border and alopecia fit perfectly. 

2. **Tinea Capitis (Ringworm):** Can cause alopecia and scaling, but the **pebbly texture is not typical**. It usually presents with broken hairs, black dots, scaling, or inflammation (kerion), which aren't described. The color (yellowish-flesh) also isn't classic. 

3. **Alopecia Areata:** Causes smooth, well-demarcated bald patches, **lacking the raised, pebbly texture** described. The skin looks normal, not thickened or yellowish. 

4. **Psoriasis:** Scalp psoriasis causes thick, scaly, red/silver plaques. **Pebbly texture isn't characteristic**, and alopecia is uncommon unless severely scratched. The color (yellowish-flesh) doesn't fit. 

5. **Scarring Alopecias (e.g., Lichen Planopilaris):** Cause permanent hair loss but typically show **perifollicular scaling, erythema, or smooth atrophy**, not a pebbly surface. Borders are often less irregular. 

6. **Cutaneous Mastocytoma:** Can have a pebbly surface but is usually **brownish**, and rubbing causes urtication (Darier's sign), not mentioned here. Alopecia isn't typical.

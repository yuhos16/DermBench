Next, I analyze the lesion itself: 1. **Raised Nature:** It's elevated, indicating a growth or nodule rather than a flat macule. 2. **Color:** Brownish-red is common in several benign lesions (like dermatofibromas, some nevi) and also in inflamed or vascular lesions. It's not described as uniformly dark black (worrying for melanoma) or pearly (typical of basal cell carcinoma). 3. **Texture:** Irregular, slightly rough/bumpy texture suggests a fibrotic or hyperkeratotic component. This rules out smooth lesions like cysts or typical lipomas. 4. **Border:** Well-defined borders are generally more reassuring than indistinct or irregular borders often seen in melanoma. 5. **Hair Presence:** Fine hairs growing *through* the lesion is a **highly significant feature**. Malignant lesions typically destroy hair follicles. The persistence of hairs strongly indicates a benign process originating in the dermis that doesn't disrupt the overlying epidermis and follicles. 6. **Location:** Extremity is a classic location for certain benign tumors. 7. **Surrounding Skin:** The dry/scaly skin might be unrelated or could represent a mild reactive change around the lesion. 

Considering key differentials: 
* **Nevus (Mole):** Can be raised and brown, but texture is usually smoother, and hairs are common. The rough/bumpy texture described is less typical. 
* **Seborrheic Keratosis:** Often rough ("stuck-on"), waxy, and brown, but hairs do not typically grow through them; they sit on the surface. 
* **Keloid/Hypertrophic Scar:** Raised and firm, but usually pink/red initially, evolving to brown, and often follows trauma. Texture can be smooth or irregular. Hairs are sparse or absent within them. 
* **Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC):** Can be nodular and brownish (pigmented BCC), but typically has a pearly edge, telangiectasias, and destroys hair follicles (no hairs growing through). 
* **Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC) / Keratoacanthoma:** Often scaly/crusted and can be nodular, but typically grow faster and destroy follicles (no hairs). Keratoacanthoma has a central keratin plug. 
* **Melanoma:** Highly variable, but irregular borders, color variation, rapid change are hallmarks. Destroys follicles (no hairs). 
* **Dermatofibroma:** Fits very well: Classic presentation is a firm, button-like, dermal nodule on extremities. Color ranges from pink/red to brown. Surface is often slightly scaly or hyperkeratotic, and texture is characteristically firm and sometimes dimples when pinched. **Crucially, hairs frequently persist and grow through dermatofibromas** because they arise in the dermis without destroying the overlying epidermis. The surrounding skin can sometimes appear slightly hyperpigmented or scaly. 

The combination of a **firm, raised, brownish-red nodule with a rough texture, well-defined borders, located on an extremity, and crucially, with hairs growing through it**, points strongly towards **dermatofibroma**. The surrounding dry/scaly skin may be incidental or mild local xerosis. 

Based on the description, the prominent lesion is most consistent with a **dermatofibroma**. This is a very common, benign growth of the skin. However, any new or changing skin lesion should be evaluated by a dermatologist in person for a definitive diagnosis.
